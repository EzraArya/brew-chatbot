# Grind Size Guide

## Overview
Grind size dictates the surface area of coffee exposed to water, acting as the primary controller for extraction speed and flavor. Getting the grind size correct is the most crucial step in brewing; even the best beans and perfect water cannot save a cup brewed with the wrong grind profile.

---

## Grind Size Calibration

### Best For
- Matching the correct grind to your chosen brew method
- Troubleshooting extraction issues (sour vs bitter)

### Parameters
| Parameter (Method) | Recommended Value (Visual Cue) |
|--------------------|--------------------------------|
| Cold Brew          | Extra Coarse (Peppercorns)     |
| French Press       | Coarse (Sea Salt)              |
| Chemex             | Medium-Coarse (Rough Sand)     |
| Kalita / Drip      | Medium (Standard Sand)         |
| V60 / AeroPress    | Medium-Fine (Table Salt)       |
| Espresso           | Fine (Powdered Sugar / Flour)  |

### Steps
1. Identify your brew method and look up the target visual cue from the parameters table.
2. Set your grinder to the middle of the recommended range for that method.
3. Grind a small amount (2g) and rub it between your fingers to check consistency and texture.
4. Brew a test cup using your standard water ratio and time.
5. Taste the cup: if it is sour, your grind is too coarse. If it is bitter, your grind is too fine.
6. Make micro-adjustments on the grinder until the flavor balances out.

### Tips
- **Fines and Boulders:** No grinder is perfect. They all produce some microscopic "fines" and large "boulders." High-quality burr grinders minimize this spread, creating a more uniform particle size.
- Grind sizes are relative; "Setting 15" on a Baratza Encore is not the same physical size as "Setting 15" on a Fellow Ode. Always calibrate by taste and visual cue.

### Common Mistakes
- **Mistake**: Using a blade grinder. Blade grinders chop coffee randomly, creating a chaotic mix of boulders (which under-extract and taste sour) and dust (which over-extract and taste bitter). Fix by upgrading to a conical or flat burr grinder.

## Grinder Maintenance

### Best For
- Extending the life of your burr grinder
- Preventing stale flavors from contaminating fresh beans

### Parameters
| Parameter       | Recommended Value     |
|-----------------|-----------------------|
| Deep Clean      | Every 1-2 months      |
| Quick Brush     | Weekly                |
| Cleaning Pellets| Every 2 months        |
| Burr Lifespan   | ~500 lbs of coffee    |
| Moisture        | 0% (Keep perfectly dry)|
| Hopper Wipe     | Weekly (Microfiber)   |

### Steps
1. Unplug the grinder and remove the hopper.
2. Use a dry microfiber cloth to wipe the coffee oils out of the hopper.
3. Remove the upper burr (check manual for specific unlatching mechanism).
4. Use a stiff nylon brush to sweep grounds out of the burr teeth and the lower chute.
5. Use a vacuum hose to suck out any remaining fines from the chamber.
6. Reassemble carefully, ensuring the burrs are seated perfectly flat before locking.

### Tips
- Never use water to clean steel burrs. Water causes immediate rust, ruining the burrs permanently.
- For deep cleaning without disassembly, run a capful of specialized grinder cleaning tablets (like Grindz) through the machine, followed by a handful of cheap coffee to purge the tablet dust.

### Common Mistakes
- **Mistake**: Adjusting the grind size finer while the grinder is off. If beans are stuck between the burrs, turning the dial finer can jam the motor or damage the adjustment ring. Fix by having the grinder motor running while adjusting finer.
