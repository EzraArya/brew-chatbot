# Common Coffee Problems

## Overview
Brewing coffee is a balancing act of extraction. We want to extract around 18-22% of the coffee bean's mass. If we extract too little (under-extraction), we get sour, weak flavors. If we extract too much (over-extraction), we get bitter, dry flavors. Troubleshooting involves adjusting variables to push extraction back into the sweet spot.

---

## Over-Extraction (Bitter/Dry)

### Best For
- Diagnosing coffee that tastes harsh, burnt, or leaves a dry, astringent feeling in the mouth (like over-steeped black tea).
- Fixing dark roasts that easily over-extract.

### Parameters
| Parameter       | Recommended Value     |
|-----------------|-----------------------|
| Target Yield    | 18-22% Extraction     |
| Adjust Grind    | Coarser               |
| Adjust Temp     | Lower (-2°C)          |
| Adjust Ratio    | Shorter (Less water)  |
| Adjust Time     | Faster pour/steep     |
| Agitation       | Decrease (Stir less)  |

### Steps
1. **Identify the taste:** Is it unpleasantly bitter, lingering on the back of the tongue? Does it make your mouth feel dry?
2. **First Fix:** Grind coarser. This reduces the surface area, slowing down the extraction of bitter compounds.
3. **Second Fix:** Lower the water temperature (e.g., from 95°C to 90°C), especially if using dark roasted beans, which are highly soluble.
4. **Third Fix:** Reduce agitation. Stop stirring the bloom or swirling the V60 too aggressively.
5. Brew a new cup changing *only one* of the variables above to see the effect.

### Tips
- Bitterness masks origin flavors. If you can't taste the "blueberry" notes printed on the bag, you are likely over-extracting.
- Dark roasts naturally taste slightly bitter due to the roast level. Keep temperatures low (80-85°C) to mitigate this.

### Common Mistakes
- **Mistake**: Assuming the coffee is "too strong" when it's actually over-extracted. People often fix bitter coffee by using less coffee grounds. Using less coffee actually *increases* extraction on the remaining grounds, making it *more* bitter. Fix by grinding coarser, not using less coffee.

## Under-Extraction (Sour/Weak)

### Best For
- Diagnosing coffee that tastes sharp, salty, overly acidic, or grass-like.
- Fixing light roasts that are dense and hard to extract.

### Parameters
| Parameter       | Recommended Value     |
|-----------------|-----------------------|
| Target Yield    | 18-22% Extraction     |
| Adjust Grind    | Finer                 |
| Adjust Temp     | Higher (+2°C)         |
| Adjust Ratio    | Longer (More water)   |
| Adjust Time     | Slower pour/steep     |
| Agitation       | Increase (Stir more)  |

### Steps
1. **Identify the taste:** Is it lip-puckeringly sour? Does the flavor vanish instantly after swallowing? Does it taste slightly salty or grassy?
2. **First Fix:** Grind finer. This increases surface area and exposes more of the bean to the water, accelerating extraction.
3. **Second Fix:** Increase water temperature (e.g., to 98°C or straight off the boil). Light roasts require massive heat energy to extract sweetness.
4. **Third Fix:** Increase the ratio. Try a 1:17 ratio instead of a 1:15 ratio. The extra water helps tame sharp acidity and round out the cup.
5. Change one variable and re-test.

### Tips
- Do not confuse "sourness" (a defect of under-extraction) with "acidity" (a positive, bright, fruit-like quality in good coffee). Sourness makes you wince; acidity makes your mouth water.
- If your pour-over drains too fast (under 2 minutes), you are almost certainly under-extracting. 

### Common Mistakes
- **Mistake**: Using water that is too cool. Many people fear "burning" coffee with boiling water. You cannot burn coffee with water (it was roasted at over 200°C/400°F). If your light roast is sour, use boiling water.
